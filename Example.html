Apüshii, the Serpent of Dreams

Theme: Respect for the invisible and ancestral wisdom

In the depths of the Guajira desert, where the wind whistles through tall cacti and the sun sets over the sand, lived a legendary serpent named Apüshii.

One night, a boy named U’uchii had a very strange dream: he was walking on a red cloud, seeing the desert from the sky. When he woke up, he saw a mark in the sand next to his hammock: a serpent’s trail that hadn’t been there the night before.

“Apüshii has come for you,” his grandmother told him. “When the serpent appears, it’s because you must learn something that cannot be taught with words.”

From that day on, U’uchii began to have visions: he could see water beneath the ground, hear the voices of the wind, and understand the songs of the birds. Everyone thought he was sick, but his grandmother cared for him and told him:

“You’re listening to what the eyes cannot see. That is what it truly means to be Wayuu.”

One afternoon, during a great drought, U’uchii said that Apüshii would guide them to water. Everyone doubted him, but they followed. He walked among dry cacti, sang ancient words in Wayuunaiki, and finally, Apüshii appeared: a great earth-colored serpent gliding toward a rock.

U’uchii struck the rock with a staff his grandfather had given him, and a stream of clear water burst forth.

From then on, U’uchii was known as a pütchipü’ü, a sage who listens to invisible messages. He learned that Apüshii was not dangerous, but protective. And that dreams, when they come from the desert, always carry a message.

“The serpent does not bite those who listen with the heart.”
